## Monetary Policy: A Pivot Toward Accommodation?

The RBI's maintenance of the repo rate at 5.25% with a neutral policy stance reflects a holding pattern that is now entering a critical juncture. With headline CPI inflation at 3.40%—well below the RBI's 4.0% midpoint and comfortably within the 2.0–6.0% target band—the central bank has created the policy space for a downward rate cycle.

The real policy rate (repo rate minus inflation) now stands at approximately 1.85%, a level that constrains credit growth in an economy that has shown signs of moderating growth momentum. The neutral stance signals the RBI's readiness to pivot toward easing, contingent on inflation remaining anchored and no external demand shocks that would destabilize the rupee.

The divergence with the US Federal Funds Rate (3.75%) is notable. The 150 basis-point spread in India's favour increases the relative attractiveness of rupee assets for global carry trades, yet the sharp FPI outflows suggest that yield pickup alone is insufficient compensation for geopolitical uncertainty and the rupee's vulnerability to crude oil and external demand shocks. We anticipate the RBI may begin a gradual rate-reduction cycle in the second half of fiscal 2026–27, conditional on inflation remaining benign and food-price pressures receding.

## Inflation Landscape: Benign, but Watch the Monsoon

At 3.40%, headline CPI inflation is at a five-year low, reflecting the RBI's credible disinflation framework and a disinflationary global environment. The figure sits well within the RBI's comfort band and provides the central bank with ample policy latitude.

Core inflation—the stickier, non-food, non-energy component—has decelerated to levels not seen since 2021, signalling that demand-side pressures are waning. However, the inflation trajectory hinges critically on three variables: monsoon performance, food-price volatility, and the path of crude oil prices. The current crude oil level of USD 120.3/barrel, while elevated in absolute terms, has stabilized after Q3 2025 volatility. A monsoon failure could rekindle vegetable and cereal price pressures, pushing CPI toward 4.5–5.0% in the October–November 2026 period.

Our base case is that inflation will remain range-bound between 3.2% and 4.5% through the fiscal year, providing the RBI sufficient cover to ease by 100–150 basis points over the next two to three quarters. The risk scenario—a monsoon deficit coupled with rising geopolitical crude tensions—would force a pause in rate cuts and could even require the RBI to maintain rates through early 2027.

## Fixed Income & Debt Markets: Term Premium Tightening, Opportunity Emerging

The 10-year G-Sec yield of 6.95% reflects a compressed term premium of 170 basis points over the repo rate. This spread is historically lean, implying that duration investors are not being adequately compensated for the inflation and interest-rate risk embedded in longer-dated bonds. For context, a "normal" term premium—in a neutral policy environment—typically ranges between 200 and 250 basis points.

The compression is symptomatic of a bond market already pricing in a rate-cut cycle. For conservative investors, the current 6.95% yield offers adequate real returns (approximately 3.55% in real terms, net of the 3.40% inflation rate), yet the term premium tightness suggests that duration extension may offer only moderate price upside unless the RBI's rate-cut trajectory accelerates markedly.

**SBI 1-year FD rates at 5.90%** present a more attractive trade-off for liquidity-conscious investors. The 105 basis-point spread over FD yields to G-Sec yields indicates that a 3-to-5-year G-Sec ladder would offer superior returns compared to rolling over 1-year FDs, conditional on a rate-cut cycle materializing. We recommend a barbell approach: preserve liquidity with short-dated FDs (6–12 months) while selectively extending into 3-to-5-year government securities to capture potential capital appreciation if the RBI delivers cuts as anticipated.

Bond funds and debt mutual funds focusing on the 3-to-5-year segment are likely to deliver better risk-adjusted returns over a 12-month horizon compared to ultra-short-duration or liquid funds, which are now yield-starved at current repo rates.

## Equity Market Valuations: Tale of Two Markets

### Nifty 50: Fair Value Zone, but Vulnerable to Flows

The Nifty 50 index, at a trailing P/E of 20.9x, sits at the upper boundary of the fair-value band (18x–22x). While the valuation is defensible given India's medium-term growth trajectory and the large-cap companies' earnings resilience, the index is vulnerable to multiple compression if foreign investors persist in selling on rupee weakness and FPI outflows.

At current valuations, a 5–8% correction in the Nifty 50 would bring the index into the cheap zone (sub-19.5x), creating a compelling entry point for systematic investors. The large-cap segment remains the defensive option in a mixed-sentiment environment.

### Nifty Midcap 150 & Nifty Smallcap 250: Expensive, Correction Likely

The Nifty Midcap 150 at 33.5x earnings is **unambiguously expensive** relative to a fair-value band of 25x–28x. Similarly, the Nifty Smallcap 250 at 29.7x is **moderately expensive** relative to a fair-value band of 20x–24x. Both segments have experienced sustained outperformance over the past 12–18 months on the back of strong domestic growth and the unwind of China-related hedges, but the valuation extension has now priced in most near-term positive surprises.

This valuation divergence—with large-cap earnings more defensively valued than mid and small caps—suggests a period of valuation correction in the lower market-cap segments is imminent. We would counsel caution toward new mid-cap and small-cap allocations at current prices unless investors have a multi-year horizon and high conviction in specific thematic or sector-driven narratives.

## Global Macro & Commodities: Crude Oil, Currency, and Gold Dynamics

### Brent Crude: Inflation Headwind, But Stabilizing

Brent crude at USD 120.3/barrel remains elevated and carries a material imported-inflation component for India. Every USD 10 increase in crude oil translates to a 15–20 basis-point inflation impact over a 12-month lag, primarily through fuel and fertilizer costs. At the current level, crude is contributing approximately 40–50 basis points to the headline CPI trajectory, reducing the RBI's easing headroom.

However, crude has stabilized from the USD 135–140 range seen in early 2025. Unless a major geopolitical escalation (Middle East conflict, sanctions on Russian supply) materializes, we expect crude to oscillate between USD 110 and USD 130 through 2026. A sustained move above USD 130 would force the RBI to recalibrate its rate-cut timing and magnitude.

### Gold: Rupee Weakness, Not Commodity Strength

Gold at ₹150,440 per 10g (USD 4,579/troy oz) is elevated, but the price action is overwhelmingly driven by rupee depreciation to 94.85 rather than genuine global gold-price momentum. In USD terms, gold is only marginally above the USD 4,500 level seen six months ago. The INR depreciation—a function of crude oil outflows and FPI selling—has mechanically inflated the rupee gold price.

For investors, this distinction matters: gold is not signalling a broad commodity reflation or heightened geopolitical risk premium (both of which would justify higher allocations). Instead, it reflects currency weakness. We recommend maintaining gold at 5–7% of portfolio allocation, viewing it as a rupee-hedge and inflation-insurance tool rather than a core return driver.

## Foreign Flows & Currency: A Headwind to Monitor

The ₹43,967 crore FPI outflow in the latest month is a material negative signal. Foreign investors have become net sellers for three of the last four months, a reversal from the consistent inflows of 2024. The drivers are multifaceted: (i) the attractive USD carry trade has reduced the relative appeal of rupee assets, (ii) elevated crude prices and rupee depreciation have signalled imported-inflation risk, and (iii) China's incremental stimulus measures have pulled capital back to Asian ex-India names.

The USD/INR at 94.85 represents a 4–5% depreciation versus the November 2025 low of 90.50. While RBI intervention has been steady (likely buying dollars on dips to prevent sharper depreciation), the central bank's foreign reserves, while adequate at USD 720+ billion, are being deployed defensively.

We expect the RBI to maintain a managed float, allowing the rupee to weaken modestly (to 95.5–96.5) rather than defend a hard level, in order to preserve reserves for genuine crisis moments. The carry-trade narrowing and FPI outflows create a near-term headwind for equity valuations, likely to persist through May–June 2026 unless global monetary conditions ease or crude oil rolls over sharply.

## Investment Implications & Outlook

### Large-Cap Equity (Nifty 50)

**Verdict:** Fair value with downside risk; suitable for **moderate and aggressive** investors via systematic, rupee-cost-averaging strategies.

At 20.9x trailing earnings, the Nifty 50 offers exposure to India's structural growth narrative at reasonable valuations. However, the index is vulnerable to a 5–8% correction if FPI selling persists. Conservative investors should build positions gradually over the next 2–3 months rather than deploying lump sums. Moderate and aggressive investors can accept the near-term volatility as a correction would present an attractive accumulation window.

### Mid-Cap & Small-Cap Equity

**Verdict:** Expensive; suitable for **aggressive** investors with 3+ year horizons, and only for thematic conviction bets.

A 15–25% correction in the Nifty Midcap 150 and 10–20% in the Nifty Smallcap 250 would restore fair valuations. New allocations are not justified at current prices except for investors with high conviction in sector-specific tailwinds (e.g., infrastructure, financial services for mid-cap, or consumption narratives for small-cap). Conservative investors should avoid fresh exposure.

### Debt & Fixed Income

**Verdict:** Moderately attractive; suitable for **all investor profiles**, with a 3–5 year horizon.

The 6.95% G-Sec yield offers a credible real return of 3.5% in a disinflation environment. A 100–150 basis-point rate-cut cycle over the next two to three quarters could deliver 3–5% price appreciation in the 3–5 year segment. Debt mutual funds in this maturity band are preferable to rolling 1-year FDs (5.90%) for investors who can lock in duration for 2–3 years. Conservative investors benefit from the yield floor; moderate and aggressive investors can capture potential price upside.

### Gold

**Verdict:** Maintain, do not accumulate; suitable for **conservative** investors as a hedge, and **moderate** investors as a 5–7% allocation.
