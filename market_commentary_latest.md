## Monetary Policy: A Steady Hand in Uncertain Times

The RBI has maintained its repo rate at 5.25% with a neutral policy stance—a measured posture that reflects confidence in the inflation trajectory while preserving flexibility. The 150 basis point differential between the RBI repo rate (5.25%) and the US Federal Funds Rate (3.75%) represents a meaningful positive carry for rupee-denominated assets, a historically attractive wedge that should, in principle, support foreign capital inflows.

However, the *actual* behaviour of foreign investors this month tells a different story. This divergence—between rate attractiveness and flow reality—warrants close examination. The neutral stance suggests the RBI believes there is room for cuts if inflation remains benign and growth concerns emerge. The 10-year G-Sec yield at 6.91% implies market pricing of potential rate reductions within the coming 12–18 months, a prospect that appears well-anchored given current price pressures.

The key watch remains the trajectory of US rate policy. Should the Federal Reserve signal sustained higher-for-longer rates or abandon its easing bias, the rate differential could narrow further, placing pressure on rupee carry trades and potentially extending the current outflow cycle. Conversely, if the Fed cuts more aggressively than currently expected, the India-US rate advantage could widen, restoring foreign investor appetite.

## Inflation Landscape: Anchored, but Vigilance Required

Consumer Price Inflation (CPI) stands at 3.40% year-on-year, a level that sits comfortably in the lower half of the RBI's 2–6% target band. This suggests core demand-side pressures remain muted and supply-side risks—notably food and energy inflation—have eased materially.

At this inflation level, the RBI is not constrained by upside price risks and can focus on growth considerations. The neutral stance is therefore a rational policy choice: it signals the central bank is neither tightening further nor rushing to cut, but rather waiting for clearer signals on the growth–inflation trade-off.

For investors, this environment is favourable for both equity and fixed-income positioning, as it removes the tail risk of emergency rate hikes. The inflation-real growth equation appears balanced, reducing the probability of stagflationary shocks in the near term.

## Fixed Income & Debt Markets: Real Returns at Fair Levels

The 10-year G-Sec yield at 6.91% translates into a **real yield** of approximately 3.51% (nominal yield minus CPI inflation), a level that is neither punitive for savers nor overly generous to borrowers. This represents a reasonable compensation for duration risk in a stable but uncertain policy environment.

The **term premium** (G-Sec yield minus repo rate) stands at 1.66%, reflecting the market's expectation of a gradual decline in short-term rates over the maturity spectrum. Historically, this spread has oscillated between 1.5% and 2.5% in normal markets; we are currently at the tighter end, suggesting limited further compression is likely unless the RBI surprises with aggressive cuts.

More meaningfully, the **debt-versus-FD spread** (G-Sec yield minus SBI 1-Year FD rate) is 1.01%, a modest but sustainable advantage for G-Sec holders over bank deposit investors. This spread adequately compensates for the additional liquidity and credit risk embedded in government securities, making debt funds an attractive hold for investors seeking steady, inflation-beating returns without equity market volatility.

For conservative investors, the combination of a 5.90% FD yield and capital preservation merit consideration. For moderate investors willing to accept duration risk, the 6.91% G-Sec yield offers compelling real returns, particularly if rates ease over the next 12 months.

## Equity Market Valuations: Divergence Across Market-Cap Tiers

Nifty 50 trades at a trailing PE of **21.4x**, positioning the large-cap index at the cusp of fair value. Using the analytical framework where ~18x is cheap, 20–22x is fair, and 25x+ is expensive, the Nifty 50 appears justly valued for quality and earnings stability. There is no compelling valuation case to overweight large-caps, but equally, no urgent signal to underweight.

The Nifty Midcap 150, trading at 34.7x PE, has extended well beyond fair valuation (consensus fair value ~25x). This represents a **39% premium** to fair value, a significant and concerning stretch that suggests either extraordinary earnings growth expectations or broad-based momentum buying. Given the absence of exceptional macro tailwinds, this valuation appears vulnerable to multiple compression.

Similarly, the Nifty Smallcap 250 at 27.0x PE trades above fair value (~20x) by approximately 35%, a material but less extreme overvaluation than mid-caps. Small-cap valuations often justify a premium to large-caps due to growth narratives, but current levels assume a degree of earnings certainty that is rarely guaranteed in smaller, less liquid companies.

**Valuation Assessment:** The market exhibits a troubling **valuation gradient** favouring smaller companies—precisely the inverse of prudent risk management. This is a classic behavioural market signal that often precedes corrective episodes. The concentration of expensive valuations in mid- and small-cap segments heightens portfolio concentration risk and weakens the structural foundation for equity returns.

## Global Macro & Commodities: Oil Elevated, Gold Buoyant

**Brent Crude Oil** at USD 95.4 per barrel remains elevated relative to historical averages, though below the crisis thresholds seen in prior cycles. At current pricing, imported inflation pressure on India's current account is material but manageable. Oil at USD 95/barrel translates to an approximate 7–8% import cost headwind versus a base case of USD 80–85. While not acute, this tightens the fiscal space for subsidised fuel and increases the probability of continued pass-through to consumer inflation.

For the RBI, elevated crude prices constrain the pace of rate cuts—each USD 5 swing in oil implies roughly 10–15 basis points of inflation volatility. Should crude breach USD 100, the calculus shifts materially towards caution.

**Gold** presents an intriguing dual-currency narrative. In INR terms, gold trades at ₹15,529 per 10g; in USD, at USD 4,784 per troy ounce. Converting the USD price to INR (USD 4,784 × 92.70 = ₹443,600 per 10 troy oz, or approximately ₹15,450 per 10g), the INR gold price aligns closely with the USD valuation—suggesting that **currency movements are not the primary driver** of recent gold appreciation.

Instead, the buoyancy in gold reflects genuine demand for safe-haven assets amid persistent geopolitical tensions and central bank accumulation. For Indian investors, gold at current levels offers inflation protection and portfolio diversification, particularly for conservative and moderate investors uncomfortable with equity risk at current valuations.

## Foreign Flows & Currency: A Cautionary Signal

FII/FPI flows turned decidedly negative this month, with net outflows of ₹48,213 crore. This reversal is striking given that India's macroeconomic fundamentals remain resilient and the rupee carry differential is attractive at 150 basis points above US rates.

The divergence between valuation (expensive mid- and small-caps) and foreign investor behaviour (selling) is not coincidental. Foreign institutional investors, who typically have strict valuation discipline, appear to be rotating away from expensive Indian equities—likely redeploying capital towards either (a) cheaper emerging markets, (b) higher-yield US fixed income, or (c) defensive global assets.

The USD/INR exchange rate at 92.70 reflects this reduced foreign demand. Should outflows persist, the rupee could face near-term depreciation pressure, which would further reduce the attractiveness of rupee-denominated assets in foreign-currency terms, potentially creating a negative feedback loop.

**RBI Stance:** The central bank is likely to be a passive observer of modest rupee weakness in the 92–94 range, viewing it as a natural correction from tighter-than-normal valuations. However, should the rupee approach 94, expect defensive intervention to prevent imported inflation shocks.

## Investment Implications & Outlook

### **Large-Cap Equity (Nifty 50)**
**Verdict:** *Fairly valued; selective positioning recommended.*

At 21.4x PE, the Nifty 50 offers limited upside from multiple expansion but represents reasonable value for earnings growth. For investors, this is a "hold and add on weakness" environment. Avoid aggressive overweighting; instead, maintain a benchmark-aligned or slight underweight position, using any sharp corrections (PE approaching 19–20x) as a buying opportunity.

**Suitability:** Moderate investors; core holding for conservative portfolios.

---

### **Mid-Cap & Small-Cap Equity**
**Verdict:** *Expensive; reduce exposure; tactical hedging warranted.*

The 34.7x and 27.0x PE multiples for mid- and small-caps are indefensible absent a significant acceleration in earnings growth. The foreign flow reversal (–₹48,213 crore) is a leading indicator that institutional discipline is reasserting itself. For existing holders, consider trimming positions to lock in gains. New money should avoid chasing these segments until valuations compress meaningfully (mid-cap PE towards 25x, small-cap towards 22x).

**Suitability:** Aggressive investors only; avoid for conservative and moderate portfolios.

---

### **Fixed Income & Government Securities**
**Verdict:** *Attractive real returns; upgrade allocation.*

The 10-year G-Sec yield at 6.91% and the 3.51% real yield above inflation represent compelling risk-adjusted returns. The term premium of 1.66% is adequate but not excessive, suggesting further significant compression is unlikely. Duration positioning in debt portfolios is justified, particularly for investors comfortable with modest interest-rate risk.

**Suitability:** Conservative and moderate investors; core allocation.

---

### **Bank Fixed Deposits**
**Verdict:** *Reasonable alternative to G-Secs; liquidity premium valued.*

At 5.90% for 1-year tenure, SBI FDs offer a 101 basis point spread below G-Secs but provide superior liquidity and regulatory safety perception. The FD route is sensible for investors requiring regular access to capital or those discomfited by mark-to-market risk in securities.

**Suitability:** Conservative investors; short-term liquidity needs.

---

### **Gold**
**Verdict:** *Defensive positioning; inflation hedge justified.*

Gold at current levels reflects genuine geopolitical risk premium and central bank demand, not currency weakness. Maintain a 5–10% portfolio allocation for downside protection and inflation insurance, particularly given equity valuation risks.

**Suitability:** Conservative and moderate investors; portfolio stabiliser.

---

### **Liquid Funds & Cash Equivalents**
**Verdict:** *Competitive yields; consider for short-term parking.*

With repo at 5.25%, overnight and short-term liquid fund yields offer competitive risk-free returns for surplus capital. Useful for bridging between equity allocations and fixed-income holdings.

**Suitability:** All investor profiles for tactical liquidity management.

## Concluding Observations

The Indian market at the cusp of April 2026 presents a tale of two economies: the large-cap segment, rationally priced and underpinned by stable macroeconomics; and the mid- and small-cap segments, inflated by momentum and foreign retail capital flows.

The reversal in FPI flows (–₹48,213 crore) is a sobering reminder that valuations ultimately matter. Foreign investors, with longer institutional mandates, are voting with their capital: they believe Indian equities have overextended, particularly in riskier segments.

For prudent investors, the signal is clear: **rebalance towards large-cap and fixed income; reduce mid- and small-cap exposure; maintain gold for diversification.** This is not a moment for capitulation, but for disciplined risk management and selective opportunism on weakness
